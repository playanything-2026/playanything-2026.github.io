PlayAnything: Enabling Cross-Game Generalization for Agents via Multimodal Prompt Adaptation

Anonymous Authors

Abstract

Vision-language model (VLM) driven game agents have shown strong performance in specific games; however, existing frameworks require extensive manual customization for each new game, including game-specific prompts and action functions. This dependence results in near-zero cross-game generalization. To address this limitation, we propose PlayAnything, a plug-and-play multimodal prompt adaptation framework that enables VLM-based agents to generalize to diverse unseen games.

Specifically, PlayAnything consists of three complementary modules: i) Gather, a multimodal retriever that constructs structured game profiles from gameplay videos, screenshots, and strategy texts; ii) Adapter, a core module fine-tuned on Qwen3-VL-8B that transforms these profiles into executable pre-filled prompts and action functions; and iii) Guider, a suggestion module that provides targeted improvement suggestions for humans to refine the pre-filled prompts via cross-reference coverage analysis.

Experiments demonstrate that PlayAnything, trained on only 5 games, achieves 100% runnability across 40 unseen games. Even without any human refinement, PlayAnything attains an average success rate of 58%, substantially outperforming existing baselines: Cradle and CombatVLA both achieve 0%, while the much larger NitroGen reaches only 42%. Furthermore, with guidance from the Guider module, humans need to make a median of only 136 character-level modifications to the pre-filled prompts, which further increases the average success rate to 67.5%.

  1. PlayAnything overcomes the zero-generalization barrier. Both Cradle and CombatVLA achieve 0% Runnability (i.e., SR1), indicating that modular frameworks and specialized VLAs fail to launch unseen games or transfer skills across types. In contrast, PlayAnything achieves 100% Runnability across all 40 test games by automatically generating action functions and prompts.
  2. PlayAnything outperforms large-scale foundation models with a fraction of the data. Compared to NitroGen (pre-trained on 40,000 hours of gameplay), our PlayAnything framework (trained on only 5 games) elevates the overall average success rate from 42.0% to 67.5%. This advantage is particularly evident in Coordinate Control games, where NitroGen collapses to near 25.8% due to its rigid gamepad primitives, whereas PlayAnything adaptively grounds interaction logic in visual coordinates to maintain robust generalization.
  3. Guider-directed refinement unlocks peak performance with minimal human effort. With lightweight strategic intervention (median Edit Distance of only 136 characters), PlayAnything further pushes the Level 2 success rate to 92.0%. These results confirm that our framework effectively shifts the human role from redundant programming to high-level strategic correction, allowing the agent to resolve complex semantic bottlenecks that remain challenging for pure end-to-end models.

Conclusion

In this paper, we present PlayAnything, a plug-and-play multimodal prompt adaptation framework designed to enable VLM-driven game agents to generalize across diverse unseen games without requiring labor-intensive per-game manual customization. Experiments show that, trained on only 5 games, PlayAnything reaches 100% runnability on 40 unseen games. Without human refinement, PlayAnything (w/o Guider) alone achieves 58% average success, clearly outperforming Cradle, CombatVLA, and the much larger NitroGen. With Guider, a median of only 136 character-level human edits further raises success to 67.5%, showing the practicality and efficiency of human-in-the-loop refinement.
